0. Preliminaries

AXIOM. Well Ordering Principle.
Every nonempty set of positive integers contains a smallest element.

THEOREM 0.1. Division Algorithm.
Let $a$ and $b$ be integers with $b \gt 0$. Then there exist unique integers $q$ and $r$ with the property that $a = bq + r$, where $0 \le r \lt b$.
We begin with the existence portion of the theorem.
Consider the set $S = \{a - bk\ |\ k \in\mathbb{Z}, a - bk \ge 0\}$.
If $0 \in S$, then $b$ divides $a$ and we may obtain the desired result with $q = \frac{a}{b}$ and $r = 0$.
Now assume $0 \notin S$. Since $S$ is nonempty (if $a \gt 0, a - b \cdot 0 \in S$; if $a \lt 0, a - b(2a) = a(1 - 2b) \in S; a \ne 0$ since $0 \notin S$), we may apply the Well Ordering Principle to conclude that $S$ has a smallest member, say $r = a - bq$. Then $a = bq + r$ and $r \ge 0$, so all that remains to be proved is that $r \lt b$.
If $r \ge b$, then $a - b(q + 1) = a - bq - b = r - b \ge 0$, so that $a - b(q + 1) \in S$. But $a - b(q+1) \lt a - bq$, and $a - bq$ is the smallest member of $S$. So, $r \lt b$.
To establish the uniqueness of $q$ and $r$, let us suppose that there are integers $q, q', r, r'$ such that $$a = bq + r \qquad 0 \le r \lt b$$ $$a = bq' + r' \qquad 0 \le r' \lt b$$ For convenience, we may also suppose that $r \ge r'$. Then $$bq + r = bq' + r' \implies b(q - q') = r - r'.$$ So, $b$ divides $r - r'$ and $r - r' \lt b$ ($0 \le r - r' \le r \lt b$). It follows that $r - r' = 0$, and therefore $r = r'$ and $q = q'$.

The integer $q$ in the division algorithm is called the quotient upon dividing $a$ by $b$.
The integer $r$ is called the remainder upon dividing $a$ by $b$.

DEFINITION. Greatest Common Divisor.
The greatest common divisor of two nonzero integers $a$ and $b$ is the largest of all common divisors of $a$ and $b$.
We denote this integer by $gcd(a, b)$.

DEFINITION. Relatively Prime Integers.
When $gcd(a, b)=1$, we say $a$ and $b$ are relatively prime.

THEOREM 0.2. GCD is a Linear Combination.
For any nonzero integers $a$ and $b$, there exist integers $s$ and $t$ such that $gcd(a, b) = s \cdot a + t \cdot b$.
Moreover, $gcd(a, b)$ is the smallest positive integer of the form $s \cdot a + t \cdot b$.
Consider the set $S = \{ma + nb \ | \ m, n \in\mathbb{Z}, ma + nb \gt 0\}$. Since $S$ is obviously nonempty (if some choice of $m$ and $n$ makes $ma + nb \lt 0$, then replace $m$ and $n$ by $-m$ and $-n$), the Well Ordering Principle asserts that $S$ has a smallest member, say, $d = sa + tb$. We claim that $d = gcd(a, b)$.
To verify this claim, use the division algorithm to write $a = dq + r$, where $0 \le r \lt d$. If $r \gt 0$, then $r = a - dq = a - (sa + tb)q = a - saq - tbq = (1 - sq)a + (-tq)b \in S$, contradicting the fact that $d$ is the smallest member of $S$. So, $r = 0 \implies a = dq$ and $d$ divides $a$.
Analogously (or, better yet, by symmetry), $d$ divides $b$ as well. This proves that $d$ is a common divisor of $a$ and $b$.
Now suppose $d'$ is another common divisor of $a$ and $b$ and write $a = d'h$ and $b = d'k$. Then $d = sa + tb = s(d'h) + t(d'k) = d'(sh + tk)$, so that $d'$ is a divisor of $d$. Thus, among all common divisors of $a$ and $b$, $d$ is the greatest.

COROLLARY.
Integers $a$ and $b$ are relatively prime if and only if there exist integers $s$ and $t$ such that $as + bt = 1$.

LEMMA 0.1. Euclid's Lemma.
If $p$ is a prime that divides $ab$, then $p$ divides $a$ or $p$ divides $b$: $$p|ab \implies p|a \lor p|b$$
Suppose $p$ is a prime that divides $ab$ but does not divide $a$. We must show that $p$ divides $b$. Since $p$ does not divide $a$, there are integers $s$ and $t$ such that $1 = sa + tp$: $$1 = sa + tp \implies b \cdot 1 = b \cdot (sa + tp) \implies b = bsa + btp = (ab)s + (bt)p.$$ Since $p|ab$ by the condition of the lemma then $p$ divides the right-hand side of last equation, what means $p$ also divides $b$.

THEOREM 0.3. Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.
Every integer greater than $1$ is a prime or a product of primes.
This product is unique, except for the order in which the factors appear.
That is, if $n = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_r$ and $n = q_1 q_2 \cdots q_s$, where the $p$'s and $q$'s are primes, then $r = s$ and, after renumbering the $q$'s, we have $p_i = q_i$ for all $i$.

DEFINITION. Least Common Multiple.
The least common multiple of two nonzero integers $a$ and $b$ is the smallest positive integer that is a multiple of both $a$ and $b$.
We will denote this integer by $lcm(a, b)$.
